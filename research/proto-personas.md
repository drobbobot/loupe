# Proto-Personas
_Prepared: 2026-02-25_

---

## How to Read These Personas

These are proto-personas — early-stage composites built from research rather than user interviews. They represent the three primary audience types the market landscape and foundations research has surfaced. They should be treated as hypotheses to be tested, not as fixed truths.

Each persona includes: who they are, their job-to-be-done (the real underlying goal they're hiring the product to accomplish), their current frustrations with what exists, and their ideal product experience.

---

## Persona 1: Sofía — The Coach or OD Consultant

**Who she is**

Sofía is 42. She's been an executive coach for eight years, having moved across from a career in HR and organisational development. She works mainly with senior leaders and leadership teams — a mix of retained one-to-one coaching and team facilitation work. She's based in London and works across Europe remotely.

She's a serious practitioner. She's done her ICF credentials, completed Immunity to Change facilitator training, has a passing familiarity with the Enneagram and with Laloux's work, and first encountered Spiral Dynamics two years ago through a colleague who'd done the ValueMatch Level 1 training. She immediately recognised it as one of the more powerful frameworks she'd seen — it explained things about her clients that nothing else quite captured — but she hasn't yet formalised her knowledge or invested in certification.

She runs a small practice alone. She earns well but watches her professional development budget carefully and doesn't have time for multi-week cohort programmes.

**Job-to-be-done**

*"Help me explain why this client keeps getting in their own way — and give me a shared language I can use with them without it sounding esoteric or cult-adjacent."*

Sofía's underlying job is to be more effective with harder clients — specifically those stuck in a pattern she can feel but can't quite name. She's looking for a framework that gives her diagnostic clarity and gives her clients an accessible, non-threatening way to understand themselves and the dynamics around them. She also needs the framework to be credible enough to deploy in corporate environments where "woo" will lose the room.

**Frustrations with what exists**

- The official tools (ValueMatch, NVC assessments) require expensive certification training before she can use them professionally. She doesn't object to investing but the time requirement — multi-week live cohorts — doesn't fit her schedule or her solo practice model.
- The free online quizzes feel embarrassingly shallow. She can't send a client to SDTEST.me and expect them to take the results seriously.
- The Beck & Cowan book is thorough but dense — she can't hand it to clients, and working through it herself as a practitioner guide is slow.
- When she tries to explain the framework verbally, it sounds either like astrology ("you're a Blue person") or like a ranking system ("you're at a lower level") — both of which create defensiveness.
- There's no good tool for the relational dimension. Spiral Dynamics clearly has implications for how team members interact across different levels, but she has no structured way to explore this with a leadership team.

**Ideal product experience**

Sofía wants a professional-grade tool she can use *with* clients and *about* clients. Specifically:

- A thoughtful self-assessment she can send to clients as pre-work that produces a meaningful, nuanced result — not just a colour score, but a genuine picture of their centre of gravity, their shadow tendencies, and what growth looks like from where they are
- A client-facing interface that's clean and credible enough to share in a corporate context — not spiritually coded, not academic-looking
- A practitioner layer she can access alongside the client view — contextual notes, conversation prompts, what to watch for, common misconceptions at each level
- Tools for the relational and team dimension — some way to map multiple people's orientations and help them see how they interact
- Language she can borrow and adapt — good explanations of each level she can use in coaching conversations without having to invent from scratch
- A pathway to deepen her own knowledge on her own schedule, not in a fixed cohort

She'll pay a monthly or annual subscription at a professional tier. She'd refer colleagues if it's good enough.

## Persona 2: Marcus — The Curious Self-Developer

**Who he is**

Marcus is 34. He works as a product designer at a mid-size tech company. He's articulate, curious, and has been quietly building a self-development practice for the last five years — therapy, some meditation, a lot of books and podcasts. He reads broadly: psychology, philosophy, complexity theory, the occasional bit of organisational theory when it feels relevant to his work.

He first encountered Spiral Dynamics through the Robcast's "Me, We, Everybody" series eighteen months ago. It landed hard. He listened to all four episodes twice in a week and spent a fortnight thinking about almost nothing else. He sent the podcast to three friends. He then tried to go deeper and hit a wall — the Beck and Cowan book felt impenetrable, the free online quizzes felt like a let-down, and the certification training was obviously not designed for someone like him.

He's not a coach or consultant. He's not trying to use this professionally in any formal sense. He just genuinely wants to understand the framework, understand himself within it, and use it to make sense of the relationships, conversations, and cultural dynamics he's living through.

**Job-to-be-done**

*"Give me a map that actually explains why my relationships feel the way they do — and why the world is so broken — and then show me what to do with that."*

Marcus's real underlying job is meaning-making. He's living through a period of significant professional and personal uncertainty — the post-pandemic world, AI disruption hitting his industry, the constant low-level noise of political catastrophe. The Robcast series gave him a framework that made all of this feel intelligible rather than chaotic. He wants more of that. He also wants to understand specific relationships: his Blue father, his Orange manager, his Green friendship group, his own apparent mix. The self-understanding is important but the relational understanding is where it gets practically urgent.

**Frustrations with what exists**

- The gap between the podcast and the next available resource is enormous. There's nothing between "four-episode gateway" and "read a dense academic book or pay €900 to do a certification programme." The conveyor belt doesn't exist.
- The free quizzes give him a colour and almost nothing else. No context, no nuance, no "here's what this means in practice," no "here's what to actually do."
- The books assume either a practitioner audience (Beck/Cowan) or a politically engaged reader willing to sit with Wilber's style (Trump book). Neither is quite right as a personal growth resource.
- The framework can feel dangerously prone to becoming another personality type system — he's wary of that, having watched friends reduce the Enneagram to an identity ("I'm a 4, I can't help it"). He wants something that captures the dynamic, contextual nature of the model but he doesn't know how to find it.
- Nothing he's found talks about the shadow seriously. Everyone explains what healthy Orange looks like. Nobody tells him what it looks like when he's running Orange in a stressed, closed-down way — which is exactly the self-knowledge he actually needs.

**Ideal product experience**

Marcus wants something he can explore at his own pace, that keeps rewarding him the more he puts in:

- An engaging, beautifully designed entry point — something that earns his trust immediately through quality and care, not through credentials
- A self-assessment that feels honest and sophisticated — that asks him interesting questions rather than obvious ones, and that produces a result he recognises rather than one he inflates to
- Clear, readable explanations of each level that feel alive and culturally grounded — connecting the model to things he's actually experiencing in 2026, not just abstract theory
- A relational dimension — some way to explore how different level combinations play out in actual relationships, and what that means for specific dynamics he's navigating
- A shadow layer — what does each level look like at its worst, from the inside? What are the tells? What does movement away from that look like?
- A learning pathway that goes deeper without requiring a financial or time commitment he can't make
- Something genuinely mobile-friendly — this is the kind of content he wants to return to on a commute or during a walk, not just at a desk

He'd pay for a personal tier. He'd post about it if it was good.

## Persona 3: Priya — The Leader Encountering SD Through Culture Work

**Who she is**

Priya is 51. She's the co-founder and CEO of a 120-person professional services firm. She's been running organisations for fifteen years and considers herself a thoughtful, values-led leader — she reads widely, invests heavily in culture, and has been doing executive coaching herself for the last three years.

She first encountered Spiral Dynamics eighteen months ago when her OD consultant — working with her leadership team on a culture redesign — introduced it as a diagnostic framework. The consultant used it loosely, mainly at the organisational level, drawing heavily on Laloux's Teal framing. Priya immediately wanted to go deeper than the consultant was taking her.

She's read Reinventing Organisations twice. She's started Trump and a Post-Truth World but hasn't finished it. She has a clear intuition that Spiral Dynamics is one of the most useful lenses she's encountered for understanding what's actually happening in her organisation — why certain conversations keep failing, why some team members can't seem to work together, why her leadership team's attempts at "psychological safety" culture keep producing performative Green rather than genuine trust.

**Job-to-be-done**

*"Help me understand what's actually happening in my organisation — below the surface — so I can lead change that sticks rather than change that performs."*

Priya's underlying job is effective organisational leadership in a complex, multi-level human system. She knows the org has a cultural centre of gravity somewhere between Blue and Orange, with pockets of Green in some teams and at least one aggressively Red individual in a senior role she's been managing around for two years. She wants a framework that helps her see this clearly, communicate it to her leadership team, and make better decisions about hiring, team design, conflict, and culture change.

She's also quietly trying to understand herself better — specifically, to understand whether what she experiences as "Yellow leadership" is genuine systemic thinking or a more sophisticated form of Green's consensus-seeking dressed up in integrative language. She suspects the latter and wants a tool honest enough to show her.

**Frustrations with what exists**

- The Teal/Laloux tooling (Atlas Platform, Reinventing Org Map) is entirely organisational — it can tell her where the organisation sits but says almost nothing about individual leaders or the relational dynamics between them.
- Her OD consultant uses the framework well in the room but there's no tool she can use independently — no way to do her own analysis, prepare for conversations, or track change over time without scheduling a consultant session.
- The language is a constant problem. "You're being very Blue right now" is not something she can say to her CFO. She needs a way to use the framework's insights without the framework's jargon dominating the conversation.
- The assessment tools that do exist are either too simple (free quizzes) or too gated (€900 certification training that she doesn't need — she needs to understand her people, not become a certified practitioner).
- Nothing addresses the gap between what a level looks like in theory and what it looks like in a specific organisational context. She knows what healthy Blue looks like abstractly; she needs to understand what it looks like in her finance team's approach to risk.
- She's found no resource that takes the leader's perspective seriously as a starting point — everything is either individual self-development or abstract org theory. The question of "how do I lead effectively across multiple levels simultaneously" is almost nowhere.

**Ideal product experience**

Priya wants a tool she can use strategically, independently, and with her leadership team:

- A sophisticated self-assessment that she trusts to be honest — that has enough friction to resist her obvious temptation to self-report as Yellow, and that surfaces the shadow material she suspects is there
- An organisational/team dimension — some structured way to map her leadership team's orientations and understand the dynamics between them
- Practical guidance on leading across levels — how do you communicate a change initiative to people whose centre of gravity is Blue when your change rationale makes sense from Orange? How do you hold a Red senior leader without either enabling or triggering them?
- Jargon-light language throughout — insights she can use in her own voice, without having to explain the theoretical framework every time
- A way to track change over time — is the culture actually shifting, or is it the same patterns with new language?
- A credible, shareable output — something she could use in a leadership team session that positions the framework as a serious diagnostic rather than a personality quiz

She'd pay at a team or organisational tier. She'd buy it through her L&D budget. She'd want to brief her OD consultant on it before using it with the full leadership team.

## Persona 4: Jamie — The Empathic Relationship Navigator

**Who they are**

Jamie is 38. They work in a mid-level role in the public sector — project coordination in a local council. They're thoughtful, emotionally literate, and have always been the person in their family and friend group who tries to hold things together and see multiple sides. They've done some therapy. They read broadly when something catches their attention. They're not a coach, not a business leader, not a self-development enthusiast in any formal sense — they're just someone trying to love the people in their life well and finding it increasingly hard.

The relationships putting pressure on them are specific and painful. Their dad is a retired electrician whose political views have become increasingly hard-right over the last six years — Brexit, anti-immigration, conspiratorial thinking that Jamie finds both alarming and heartbreaking. A close university friend has gone the other way entirely — intense progressive activism, a kind of moral absolutism that makes Jamie feel constantly judged and never quite good enough. And Jamie's partner has a fundamentally different relationship to risk and security that creates low-level friction in almost every financial and life decision they make together.

None of these relationships are broken. But all of them feel harder than they should. Jamie loves these people and doesn't want to give up on any of them. What they don't have is a framework for understanding *why* these conversations keep failing in the same ways.

They discovered Spiral Dynamics six months ago through the Robcast — a friend sent them the "Me, We, Everybody" series with the message "this explains everything." Jamie listened to all four episodes in two days and cried during the third one. Not because it was sad but because something finally made sense. Their dad wasn't becoming a stranger — he was speaking from a completely coherent worldview that Jamie had never been able to see as coherent before. Their friend's moral absolutism wasn't hypocrisy — it was the structure of a specific lens, with its own internal logic. The friction with their partner wasn't incompatibility — it was two centres of gravity trying to make shared decisions.

**Job-to-be-done**

*"Help me understand how the people I love actually see the world — not so I can change them, but so I can stop being baffled and hurt by them and start actually reaching them."*

Jamie's underlying job is empathic translation. They're not trying to win arguments or fix people. They're not even primarily trying to understand themselves, though that's part of it. What they're reaching for is the ability to genuinely inhabit another person's worldview long enough to understand what the world looks like from inside it — and then to find a way to be in relationship with that person that doesn't require either of them to abandon who they are.

There's a secondary layer: Jamie is also trying to understand what they need from each relationship. Not every relationship can or should survive every difference. The framework is helping them think — with more clarity and less guilt — about which relationships have a foundation that can hold, which ones need renegotiating, and which ones they may need to step back from in order to stay well.

**Frustrations with what exists**

- The podcast gave them the map but not the tools. They understand the framework conceptually but don't have any structured way to apply it to their specific relationships.
- Everything they've found since the podcast has been either too abstract (books) or too practitioner-oriented (training courses). Nothing is designed for someone trying to use this in their personal life, in real relationships, with real emotional stakes.
- The free online quizzes are almost offensive in their shallowness given the depth of what Jamie needs. Getting a colour result with two paragraphs of generic description does nothing for the question "how do I talk to my dad about what he's reading online without it becoming a screaming match?"
- They're worried about the framework becoming a tool for judgment rather than understanding. They've already caught themselves thinking "oh, he's just being Red" in a way that felt dismissive rather than compassionate. They want something that keeps the empathy at the centre and doesn't let the model become a way of categorising people from a safe distance.
- There's almost nothing in the accessible SD space that speaks to political and family estrangement specifically — which is where the framework is most urgently needed and most powerfully applicable in 2026.

**Ideal product experience**

Jamie wants a product that feels human, warm, and relationally focused — not clinical or academic:

- An entry point that validates the relational use case from the start — that speaks directly to the experience of loving someone you can't quite reach, rather than leading with self-development or professional application
- Vivid, honest portraits of each level *from the inside* — not just what it looks like from outside, but what it genuinely feels like to be someone whose centre of gravity is there. What does the world feel like from inside Blue? What does it feel like from inside Red? This is what creates empathy rather than just analysis.
- Specific relational guidance for level combinations — what does a Green-centred person actually need to understand about how to be in relationship with a Blue-centred parent? What typically goes wrong between Orange and Green in close friendships, and what does genuine bridge-building look like?
- Honest acknowledgment that some gaps can't be bridged — that the framework can help you understand *why* a relationship is painful without requiring you to fix it. A tool that helps people discern what they need, not just cope with what they have.
- Shadow material that's written with compassion rather than judgment — what does each level look like when it's frightened, defensive, or closed down? Recognising a loved one's shadow without pathologising them is a subtle skill and the product should develop it.
- A tone that feels like a wise, warm friend rather than a textbook or a coaching platform — accessible, emotionally intelligent, and respectful of the genuine difficulty of what Jamie is trying to do

They'd pay a personal tier subscription. They'd refer it to friends going through similar experiences. They'd likely be the most emotionally engaged user in the product — the one who reads every word and returns to it at difficult moments.

## Cross-Persona Patterns

Four patterns appear across all personas and should directly shape product design:

**Pattern 1: The credibility bar is high.** All four have been burned — by shallow quizzes, esoteric language, or tools that felt like toys. The product needs to earn trust through quality before it asks for anything. The first impression must feel serious.

**Pattern 2: The shadow is the most urgent unmet need.** In different ways, all four are trying to understand dysfunction — their own, their clients', their colleagues', their loved ones'. Healthy expressions of each level are relatively covered in existing resources. Shadow, unhealthy expression, and what movement looks like from a stuck place is where the gap is widest and the urgency is highest.

**Pattern 3: The relational dimension is what makes it practically useful.** Understanding your own level in isolation is interesting. Understanding how your level interacts with someone else's — in a specific relationship, in a team, in a cultural context — is where the framework becomes genuinely useful. Every persona is reaching toward this and finding nothing adequate.

**Pattern 4: The entry point is emotional, not intellectual.** None of these four personas arrived at Spiral Dynamics through academic interest. They arrived through a specific experience of confusion, pain, or bewilderment — a relationship that kept failing, a world that stopped making sense, an organisation that couldn't change, a client they couldn't reach. The product's on-ramp must meet people in that emotional place, not abstract them out of it.

**Pattern 5: Engagement is not optional — it's the product.** All four personas live in the same attention economy. They scroll. They have fifteen tabs open. They've been burned by courses they bought and never finished. The moment content becomes tedious — dense paragraphs, dry theory, abstract examples — they leave. The product must earn continued attention on every screen, not just the first. This is not a UX nicety; it is a core delivery challenge. Ideas need to land in seconds, not paragraphs. Depth must be offered in layers, not dumped upfront. Interaction, visual rhythm, and genuine surprise are not decorative — they're what makes learning stick in a world of competing signals.
